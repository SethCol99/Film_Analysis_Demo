Cinematic/Scene Analysis - Rear Window by Alfred Hitchcock

https://www.theaceblackblog.com/2012/01/movie-review-rear-window-1954.html

Significant Cinematic Elements Used

Hitchcock's film Rear Window features heavy use of editing to connect Jeff's highly confined apartment life, intimately, to the rest of the world. One of the most common techniques is the many point of view shots of smaller moments such as Doyle looking at Lisa's night bag and Jeff watching the light under the door disappear as Lars arrives. More significant instances include the multitude of occurrences when a medium shot of Jeff looking right passed the camera is juxtaposed with a shot of the particular neighbor he is looking at. The corresponding shots of his neighbors are either long, or also close ups if h's looking through binoculars or his camera. This very repetitive point of view editing is used to bring focus and emphasis to his observations despite them being relatively far away.

The film also seeks to maintain continuity and logic when the shots normally used would cause disorientation. An example of this is when Jeff is sleeping and can't look through his window at what is happening. The classic point of view shot we would normally see would't make sense in this case since Jeff is sleeping and we can't logically tie his sight to anything outside. However, the camera is used as a narrator when it pans over showing the outside where Jeff would normally look, which continues the narration of what's outside.

The film has various paces as well, with slower pacing in more dialogue based scenes like when Jeff is describing his job experiences to Lisa. We then see quicker pacing in the fight scene at the end between Jeff and Lars to convey excitement and chaos. This scene is also edited in parallel with shots of the neighbors and police rushing to the fight, bringing a relationship to the two actions and building suspense as the opposite forces come together. The film also has narrative parallels to Notorious in the way that a man is leading the mission/story and is joined by a female who normally wouldn't be in the picture. Whether the man wants the woman there or not is questionable, but there is a romantic element to the narrative and at the end of the day, the two must work together.

An Important Scene - Jeff Uncovers Lars' Truth

http://throughthereels.blogspot.com/2015/09/afi-top-100-48-rear-window.html

The scene begins with Lisa, Stella, and Jeff investigating the flowers right after the dig is found dead. The scene begins with a medium two shot behind Lisa, Stella and Jeff, showing them all looking out the window and serves as an establishing shot.

The scene is shown in low key lighting with the room being mostly dark, but the apartments across from them are better lit. This contrast conveys, rather literally, that the rest of the apartments are in their normal, everyday existence, and the three are hiding in the shadows, secretly watching over them. It can also mean that they know the darkness that's contained on that side of the courtyard, but they're bringing into the light, like they're “shedding light” on the situation with this new discovery about the dog.

we then get brought to the front of them, in a medium shot to the right which we see them looking across, and Jeff is looking through his camera lens, which sets up a pov shot that follows when the camera does a cut to Lars wiping his wall down

Again, there is a very strong contrast between the darkness on the outside of the apartment and the inside where Lars is. The window is also very small and there is little view into his room compared to the other apartments. Such a spacial contrast may be symbolic for how everyone else (having much larger access to outside) is more free and Lars is seen as more confined, like he's being closed in on and trapped, almost like his room is a prison - which he's on his way to.

We are brought back to the three in Jeff's apartment looking out in the same mid shot style as before, then the camera cuts to a close up of Lisa and Stella, isolating them from Jeff. The isolation is not just because they'e having a conversation about Stella's theory of Lars wiping blood off the wall, but also by juxtaposing this shot with an isolating shot of Jeff, which we see right after, a more significant point of view shot is set up when Jeff leans up and looks as though he sees something strange outside.

The pov shows a shot of the flowers that the dog dug through, and this sparks a frenzy in Jeff as he feels he's realizing something. The camera is currently in a close up, just showing Jeff as before, but then cuts back to a medium shot being more inclusive and showing all three again as Lisa and Stella prepare to see what Jeff is seeing.

This shot includes a deep space composition showing Lisa grab Jeff's viewfinder, and once she gives it to him, he loads the viewfinder with a picture of the flowers and alternates between looking at the picture and the actual flowers.

He hands Lisa the viewfinder so she can see the difference, and she does the same thing. She looks through it, and Hitchcock uses the Kuleshov effect when he juxtaposes this pov image with a similar shot of the flowers from earlier, to give us context for the picture of the flowers and the real thing as Lisa switches between the viewfinder and reality.

Also Jeff is telling her to look at the picture and switch back to the real flowers, while she does it. The combination of her switching and him telling her to, along with Lisa doing so and the camera showing this through Lisa's pov almost provides an indirect narration from Jeff, as if we are Lisa (since she is who we are identifying with here) and Jeff is walking us through the process.

He does the same thing with Stella, then promptly points out that something must be under the flowers since they've clearly been removed and put back. Lisa suspects that it's Lars' wife's body. Stella quickly shuts this down stating that there's no way her body would fit there, and says she thinks the body is cut and scattered around town. Jeff jumps in and confidently restates that something is under the flowers. He and Lisa then conclude it may be the tools Lars used to kill and dispose of his wife with.

This conversation about tools takes place on a different vertical third of the frame that Stella is on, which allows her to be more or less isolated in the frame, keeping her separate from the conversation and conveying her looking outside, possibly noticing something striking, which she does, and alerts Jeff about it, making it a closed frame observation. It also confirms we are currently identifying with Jeff since he isn't looking and neither are we. When Jeff looks, another pov cut shows Lars packing a suitcase, and Jeff is immediately onto something, commanding the two to get him a pencil and paper to write on.

As they're frantically gathering around a table with the pencil and paper, a zoom in brings us closer to them, a shottype that tells us something is going on and a major shift in the situation is about to happen.

We then see a high angle shot looking down at them and the table from the ceiling as Jeff begins writing. He starts with Lars' name on an envelope, then writes, asking Lars what he did with his wife, on the paper. As he writes the question, the camera, once again, zooms in on the paper, continuing the significance of this moment bringing more specified attention to the question, as well as giving us the ability to see what he's writing. The slow revelation of the question may also be symbolic of how the story of the murder is slowly getting revealed.

Jeff places the note in the envelope and the screen fades to black.

This scene not only gives a new side to Jeff's investigation, it even keeps the search for the truth alive. He stated right before this scene that he was just starting to be convinced by Doyle that he was wrong about his suspicion. However, he now has new confidence and leads into the murder, and the search keeps going.

Response to the Hitchcock/Truffaut Discussion on this Film:

Hitchcock used James in a very creative way in that he portrayed him as a total Peeping Tom, which he said raised complaints, specifically by Critic of the London Observer. However, this didn't change Hitchcock's mind, if anything, it reinforced the fact that, even if it's wrong on the screen, it's representative of everybody in the same situation. He states himself that most people who see a woman undressing or a man puttering around his room, would stare instead of saying “it's none of my business”. People will complain about this, but we all do it. He also mentions the Kuleshov effect with the specific shot of Stewart looking at the dog, and how if there was a “...half naked girl exercising in front of her open window, and you go back to a smiling Stewart again, this time he's a dirty old man” (H/T, 216). The Kuleshov effect takes place throughout the film, mainly, since there are so many instances of pov shots and matching these with a following expression imply the expression holds the emotion the character feels after seeing what they saw in the point of view.
